# The Farmer — Table of Contents

---

## [Chapter 0: The View from the Field (Before the Bell)](chapters/CHAPTER_00.md)
In the predawn mist at Muddy Gate Farm, draft horses Barnaby and Blue stand in quiet communion, steady and purposeful. When the farmhouse light flickers on, the anxious farmer — once an optimistic lad, now worn down by years of "optimizing" the impossible — approaches with an unfamiliar third harness. The horses sense something is off: they only ever needed two.

---

## [Chapter 1: The Slow Morning at Muddy Gate Farm](chapters/CHAPTER_01.md)
Running behind for Tuesday market, the farmer grows impatient with the deliberate pace of Barnaby and Blue, his cold-blood draft horses. His neighbour Silas warns that speed is a fickle friend, but the farmer dismisses this. Spotting a sleek racing horse in the neighbouring paddock, he concocts a plan: combine raw power with raw speed by adding the hot-blooded Thunderbolt to the team.

---

## [Chapter 2: Bad Decision #1: Borrowing Thunderbolt](chapters/CHAPTER_02.md)
Harnessing Thunderbolt alongside the workhorses proves immediately disastrous. The racehorse surges and lurches while Barnaby and Blue maintain their steady rhythm, causing the wagon to shudder violently. Cabbages fly off the back. Old Tom watches and offers wisdom — "a plow team is a choir, not a trumpet contest" — but the farmer blames the road rather than his plan, and decides the narrow lanes through the old quarter will fix the rhythm problem.

---

## [Chapter 3: Bad Decision #2: The Twisty Lanes to Town](chapters/CHAPTER_03.md)
The "shortcut" through Coppergate Lane turns into a comedy of disasters: a flock of geese blocks the way, a tinsmith's copper pot sends Thunderbolt into a sideways leap, spring greens scatter across the cobbles, and the wagon goes sideways in an increasingly narrow lane. The team barely squeezes through a tight pinch blocked by an oncoming timber cart. Emerging battered and late, the farmer concludes what he needs is not just power and speed, but *obedience* — and spots the travelling circus on the common ground ahead.

---

## [Chapter 4: Outskirts Truce and the Circus Temptation](chapters/CHAPTER_04.md)
On the calm outer road — the sensible route he'd earlier dismissed — the farmer regains his composure and strikes a deal with circus owner Ambrose to borrow two highly trained performance horses: Cosimo (a dark bay) and Duchess (a dapple grey). Ambrose cautions that they respond to arena cues, not road commands, but the farmer brushes this off. He harnesses all five horses together and sets off. For thirty seconds, it's glorious. Then Cosimo begins to drift left.

---

## [Chapter 5: Bad Decision #3: The Dancing Disaster](chapters/CHAPTER_05.md)
Cosimo's deep-seated arena training bias pulls him inexorably leftward. Every road command triggers a classical dressage movement instead of a practical response: "RIGHT" produces a *shoulder-in* that drops the wheel in a ditch; "LEFT" yields a *travers* aimed at a hedgerow; and "MOVE" launches Duchess into a breathtaking *passage* — a slow, floating trot that advances the wagon at a quarter walking pace. Farm labourers applaud. Children gather. When the farmer erupts in exasperation, Cosimo bows. In the ensuing stillness, the farmer sees his team clearly for the first time: they aren't failing him. He assembled five different specialists and demanded the same thing from all of them at once. He quietly picks up the reins and finally asks, rather than commands.

---

## [Chapter 6: Cliffhanger at the Market Bell](chapters/CHAPTER_06.md)
The market bell begins to toll as the farmer reaches the fork in the road. The left lane is shorter but already occupied by parade stragglers, a tuba player, and a pie cart — and Cosimo's frayed near-side trace, worn down by a morning of sideways pulling, won't survive the extra strain. The right lane adds four minutes he doesn't have, but it's clear and safe. The farmer spots his most valuable buyer, Aldous Prentice, walking toward the gate. He tries to command the team forward but receives only stillness — five horses waiting for a coherent question. As the twelfth bell begins to ring, Barnaby looks steadily down the right lane. The farmer takes a breath, feels five different tensions in the reins, and his hands begin to move. *The chapter ends here.*
