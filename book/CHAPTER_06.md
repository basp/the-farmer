### Chapter 6: Cliffhanger at the Market Bell

I heard the first bell before I saw the rooftops.

It rang out from the clock tower with the unhurried authority of a thing that has been doing this since before I was born and will continue doing it long after I have sorted out my transport arrangements. The tone crossed the last half-mile of road and reached me where I sat on the bench—coat dusty, hat askew, five sets of reins distributed between two hands and my better judgement—with a clarity that I found, under the circumstances, not entirely welcome.

One.

The rooftops came into view as the wagon cleared the final bend: the gabled ridge of the mill, then the high back wall of the great hall, then—incrementally, like a stage set being assembled—the corner of the clock tower itself, its flag catching the afternoon air. The market square. I had been aiming for it since before sunrise with the determination of a man who knows exactly what he is doing, and the evidence of a man who has spent the entire day demonstrating the opposite.

Two.

I did honest arithmetic. I am capable of it; I simply do not always choose to employ it. I chose to now.

Had I left at the original hour, in the original configuration, by the original road, I would have heard the seventh bell from the square's edge—a comfortable arrival, the kind that permits a leisurely unharnessing and a civil conversation with the gate-keeper about the weather. When I had rerouted via the town's outer edge, some portion of that margin had gone, and I would have arrived at roughly the ninth. When I had stopped at the circus—I will not revisit the sequence of decisions that led to the circus; I have reviewed them at length internally and the review has not been productive—I lost the ninth and was looking squarely at the tenth. That was before the shoulder-in, before the travers, before the passage, before the bow. Before the dog.

Three. I heard the third bell as the wagon cleared the bend.

I was facing the twelfth. Possibly something after the twelfth. The clock does not negotiate; it rings, and the market closes when it closes, as it has closed every market day for the past forty years with the remorseless indifference of a mechanism that has never once cared about my situation.

I sat with this for a moment. Not with self-reproach—I have had a great deal of self-reproach today and it has not improved anything—but with the flat, unembellished honesty of a man who is simply counting. The clock was true. The morning had not been.

I shook the reins and asked the team for more pace.

---

The road forked at the old mill corner.

I know this junction the way I know the back pasture gate or the creak of the third stair—without thought, by long familiarity, the body navigating before the mind has quite caught up. I have passed this fork on every market day for eleven years, and I have always, without deliberation, taken the left lane. It is shorter by four minutes, it is less wide, and its stones are slightly more worn, which tells you everything about which choice most people make when they arrive here with a load and a deadline.

Four. I heard the fourth bell.

I halted the team at the fork and examined the choice with the attention it had, until this moment, never required.

The left lane ran alongside the guild parade route—the long, decorated street where the weavers and the dyers and the coopers had been marching since the early morning in the manner of guilds everywhere: slowly, proudly, and at great length. The parade itself had finished an hour ago; I knew this from the distant sound of the dispersing band, heard somewhere behind me in the middle portion of the morning's disasters. But *finished* is not the same as *gone*. Parades leave residue. Stragglers with banners furled and half-furled were still drifting back through the lane in the unhurried way of people who have been part of something and are not yet quite ready to return to being nobody in particular. A musician with a tuba—a tuba, not a small instrument; a tuba, a large, gleaming, sound-generating object—was walking in the same direction I wanted to go. A cart selling hot pies had pulled up alongside the guild hall to conduct its final business of the afternoon, and around it stood a loose constellation of people who had nowhere pressing to be and were proving it.

The left lane was shorter by four minutes. It was also, at this particular juncture, narrower by necessity, occupied in patches, and — I noted this with the practiced eye of a man who has spent thirty years watching Cosimo's near-side trace develop a consistent leftward lean — exactly the direction in which the leading pair of my current team were already inclined to go. Not by my invitation. By gravity. By eleven years of circling the manège. The left lane exerted on Cosimo something close to a physical pull, and Duchess, as she had done all morning, was ready to agree with him.

The right lane was wider, emptier, and entirely clear. It ran parallel to the mill's rear wall and came out at the square's side entrance, which is slightly further from the main buyer's row but is, on any rational assessment, a better road. It added four minutes. It had always added four minutes, and I had always declined it on those grounds, because four minutes is four minutes, and I am a man who does not squander time.

I will note, with the sour precision of someone doing his accounts late on a bad day, that I have squandered somewhere between three and four hours this morning on the basis of this conviction.

Five. The team stood at the fork. Cosimo had already angled left — not dramatically, but meaningfully, with the settled lean of a horse who has found his direction and considers the matter resolved. Duchess had followed, because Duchess follows Cosimo the way shadow follows substance: without decision, without question. Behind them, Thunderbolt had begun craning left as well, turning his head to look down the lane with what I can only describe as the interest of a horse who has been going the wrong way all day and is happy to continue. Barnaby and Blue — my own horses; my enormous, deliberate, unhurried horses — were looking straight down the right lane. Not pointed that way, precisely. Just *looking*. The patient, unsentimental gaze of animals that do not have opinions about roads but do appear, when you study them long enough, to have something resembling preferences.

I spotted Aldous Prentice at the far end of the square.

Or rather I spotted the back of his coat — the good dark wool one, the one he wears to market because he is a man who treats commerce as a serious occasion — and the back of his hat, and the particular set of his shoulders that belongs to a man who has finished his morning and is thinking about his afternoon. He was at the far end of the square, near the eastern gate, moving toward it with the unhurried deliberateness of a man who has made up his mind to leave but is not yet in a hurry to confirm it.

Aldous Prentice. Eleven years of reliable custom. Eleven years of arriving at my stall without theatre, surveying the load with the even gaze of a man who knows what he wants and knows what it is worth, and naming a price — *his* price — that has, in eleven years, never once been below what I had hoped for. Not haggling: *paying*. Three fence sections on his custom. The new roof-beam. The winter I did not have to borrow money from anyone, which is a winter a man remembers. He does not inspect suspiciously or ask searching questions about the quality of last month's order in the reproachful tone of a man hoping to negotiate a reduction. He arrives, surveys, pays in coin, and departs. He is, in the plainest commercial sense I know, irreplaceable.

He was not yet through the gate. He was moving toward it. I had one minute. Possibly two.

Six bells. Six bells and Aldous Prentice's back receding at a walking pace, and five horses at a fork, and the left lane shorter and wrong and Cosimo already aimed into it, and the right lane longer and right and four minutes I did not have.

I had been standing still deciding for forty seconds.

Seven bells. I was still standing still.

---

I climbed down from the bench, because the frayed trace was not going to climb down and examine itself.

I had heard the sound a moment before the seventh bell — dry, short, minimal; the sound a green twig makes when you step on it in a quiet wood, the snap of something that was carrying more than it was designed to carry. Not a catastrophic failure. Not even, yet, a failure at all. Just a sound. But I know harness the way a carpenter knows wood, and the sound a worn trace makes as it begins to part is one I have heard before and do not care to hear again mid-street.

The near-side trace on Cosimo's collar had frayed through the outer braid. Not snapped entirely — the core threads were holding, and the whole assembly was still under tension, still transmitting load, still, by the most optimistic interpretation, functional. But the outer braid had parted, and what remained was the inner threads, carrying the full strain of a five-horse span and a loaded wagon with the help of none of the braiding designed to share it.

I crouched and looked at it for a long moment.

A well-matched team distributes its load evenly across the span — each trace bears its proper portion of the wagon's weight, and the drawbar, to which all the traces connect at the front bar, receives the sum of the team's effort as a balanced, forward pull. A mismatched team — one with different gaits, different training, different instincts about direction — creates unequal tension: one horse pulling harder here, another drifting there, the whole assembly living under stresses it was not measured for. Lateral movement is the particular enemy of trace integrity. When a horse drifts sideways against the pull — as Cosimo had been doing, without malice, without rest, since the common ground — the trace does not simply bear more load; it bears load at an angle, a rotational shear that the braid is not designed to handle.

Not bad luck. This was the morning's geometry, faithfully adding up.

I had one more wrong decision's worth of trace left.

I know this because I have mended enough harness to read the remaining threads the way a doctor reads a pulse — the number of sound fibres, the degree of fraying, the direction of the stress. The near-side trace would carry me forward in a straight line. It would not survive Cosimo pulling against it through a narrower lane and around a tuba.

I stood up. I climbed back onto the bench. I picked up the reins.

Eight bells.

---

I cannot say exactly when Aldous Prentice became a man I was watching rather than a man I was moving toward. Somewhere between the seventh and eighth bells the distinction had established itself, and it was not a comfortable one.

He was still in the square — barely. I could see him now from the bench, at the angle of a man with a clear view down the right lane, which gave me a line of sight to the eastern gate and the small, dark-coated figure approaching it with the steady gait of a man who has nowhere to be until the afternoon coach. He had not yet passed through. He was perhaps a minute from passing through, if the phrase *perhaps* is being used generously.

I did the arithmetic that I had been avoiding.

Left lane: faster by four minutes. Trace probably fails in the first narrowing. If the trace fails, I am stopped in a lane occupied by a musician with a tuba, and Cosimo is loose, and Aldous Prentice goes through the gate, and I sell at end-of-day prices to whoever is left — the kind of prices a man accepts when he has no leverage and everybody can see it.

Right lane: adds four minutes. Trace survives. Wagon arrives at the side entrance one minute and change after the twelfth bell, if Aldous has not yet left — a minute and change that I do not have, by any calculation I can perform on a market day that has been mathematically hostile since before breakfast.

Standing still deciding: the forty seconds already gone had become fifty. The fifty would become sixty. Sixty was not recoverable. I had burned three hours today on shortcuts that were not short; I was now burning seconds on arithmetic I already knew the answer to.

Nine bells.

I took up the reins with the decisive authority of a man who has resolved the matter and issued the command.

---

Nothing happened.

Not quite nothing — the kind of nothing that happens when you have given an instruction and the world has declined to receive it. Barnaby shifted his weight, a small, ballast-adjusting shift, the sort of movement that is not a response to a command but a rearrangement of patience. Blue's left ear rotated — backward, forward, backward again — like a man checking a door twice. Cosimo stood with the contained stillness of a horse waiting, specifically and professionally, for something more useful than what had just been offered. Duchess held her near foreleg raised, fractionally, barely even raised — not the gathered lift of the passage, just the faintest ghost of it, the shadow the movement casts before it arrives. Thunderbolt, who had been the most volatile element of my morning and had expressed this volatility with energy and frequency — Thunderbolt, for the first time since the common ground, stood absolutely still.

Five horses. One man with two hands full of reins. The fork in the road. The sound of the eighth bell still settling.

I issued the command again. Louder. The volume of a man who has been in charge of this expedition since dawn and has not yet fully registered that volume is not, and has not been at any point today, the relevant variable.

Five horses continued to wait.

I have spent a great deal of this morning in a state of baffled exasperation at the team's behaviour, and I will not pretend that some portion of that exasperation did not attempt to surface now. But something in the particular quality of their stillness stopped it. Not disobedience. Not stubbornness. Not the theatrical resistance of an animal asserting itself. Something quieter than all of that: the honest stopping of a horse — five horses — who have received a question that does not parse.

A well-trained horse, faced with conflicting aids, will try to decode them. Conflicting aids are what horsemen call the condition in which hand, leg, weight, and voice are all sending signals at once, and the signals do not agree — one rein pulling left while the leg asks right, the voice commanding forward while the weight sits back. A schooled horse attempts, with genuine effort, to find the intended meaning among the noise. A working horse — Barnaby, Blue — defaults to its last clear instruction and waits. An exhausted or confused horse simply stops, because stopping is the one response that does not commit to any of the contradictory options being offered.

I had been giving conflicting aids since the fork. Not through the reins — I had not yet moved them deliberately in any direction. But through everything else: the hesitation in my weight, the gathering and releasing of contact through my hands, the second command louder than the first in a tone that communicated urgency without direction. I had been asking — shouting, effectively — *something,* and the something had no clear address. I had been loudest all morning on precisely the occasions I had been clearest about the least.

Stopping was not disobedience. It was the honest response to an incoherent question.

Ten bells.

---

I sat with the reins in my hands and looked at the road ahead.

Left lane: the tuba catching the afternoon light. The stragglers with their banners. The pie cart and its comfortable audience. Cosimo angled into it already, thirty degrees and patient, the near-side trace frayed to a question-mark. The left lane was not a lane. It was a way of arriving at a different disaster more quickly.

I knew this. I had known it since before I climbed down to look at the trace. I had known it, if I am being fully honest — and I am attempting, for once today, to be fully honest — since I halted at the fork and saw Cosimo aimed left with the serene certainty of an animal that has not once been wrong about which direction it intends to go.

The right lane was the right lane. It was wider. It was clear. It led to the square's side entrance, which was further from the main buyer's row by perhaps sixty yards, and it added four minutes, and those four minutes were four minutes I did not have. But I had not had many things today that I had spent minutes not having, and the morning had proven, with a thoroughness that I could no longer in good conscience dispute, that the faster-looking thing is frequently not faster, and that the thing I have been too proud to consider is usually the thing I should have done before breakfast.

Choosing the right lane meant agreeing, in action, that the shortcut was not short. It meant accepting that I would arrive, if I arrived at all, later than I had planned on a day I had already failed to plan correctly, at a price I had always been too proud to accept, to a buyer who might or might not still be at the gate. It meant that the faster-looking road, chosen eleven times in eleven years, had today had its geometry exposed.

I could not quite manage it cleanly. Not yet. There was still something in me that wanted one more calculation, one more angle, one more interpretation of the trace that made the left lane viable.

Eleven bells.

Aldous Prentice at the gate. His hand, I thought, was moving toward the latch. A man who has made up his mind, confirming it.

The trace below me, frayed. Cosimo ahead of me, aimed left. Thunderbolt still. Duchess with her ghost of a step. Barnaby and Blue looking right, with the unsentimental patience of animals that do not offer encouragement and do not require it and are simply, with every quality of their long steady natures, there.

I looked at Barnaby. He was not looking at me. He was looking down the right lane with the specific, focused, unhurried attention of a horse that knows its job and is prepared to do it. He has been looking down the right lane, I realised, since I halted. He did not crane after Cosimo. He did not angle toward the stragglers or the tuba or the pie cart. He looked right, with the unarguable directness of an animal that has no vanity and no investment in what answer I prefer.

He did not offer encouragement. He did not offer anything. He was simply there — as he had been there since sunrise, through the geese and the copper pots and the ditch and the shoulder-in and the passage and the bow — steady, available, prepared to pull whichever road was chosen, as long as the choice was actually made.

Twelve bells began to ring.

I took a breath. I looked at the reins — the full set of them, all five, distributed between my hands in the particular arrangement I had spent the morning not quite mastering. I felt the weight of the contact, the different tensions of five different animals, each of them waiting.

My hands moved on the reins.
