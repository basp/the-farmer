### Chapter 5: Bad Decision #3: The Dancing Disaster

Thirty seconds.

That is, in retrospect, the exact duration of my confidence.

For thirty seconds—perhaps thirty-five; I am being generous—five horses moved together as a coherent unit down the road toward market square. Cosimo and Duchess led with the polished, light-footed authority of their breeding, Barnaby and Blue settled into their deep, tectonic *draft* behind them, and even Thunderbolt managed a creditable walk without shying at anything. The harness sat even. The wagon rolled true. The whole arrangement had the feel of a thing that had finally been correctly assembled.

Then Cosimo began to drift left.

It was not a sudden movement—nothing so dramatic as a shy or a spook. It was quieter than that, and in some ways worse. It was the slow, patient, entirely inevitable leftward inclination of a horse whose entire working life had been organised around a circle. In the *manège*—the enclosed training arena—a circus horse works predominantly in one rotational direction, building muscle and habit in a long, continuous curve. Years of this work create what horsemen call an *arena training bias*: one side of the horse becomes slightly stronger, slightly more conditioned, and the horse, in the absence of a strong counter-instruction, will always drift back toward the direction it knows best.

For Cosimo, that direction was left.

He did not charge left. He did not lurch. He simply leaned—a fraction of a degree, then two, then three—with the calm, geological patience of something that cannot be argued with, only redirected.

I redirected him. He straightened. Thirty seconds passed. He began again.

Duchess, responding to the subtle change in trace tension with the precision of an instrument finely calibrated to the signals of a partner, mirrored him. She was not drifting from habit—she was following what her *schooling* told her was a cue. In the arena, when Cosimo moved, it meant something. It still meant something now. She simply didn't know what it meant on a road.

The wagon's front left wheel had begun to describe a very gradual acquaintance with the grass verge.

"Right rein," I muttered, and adjusted. The pair straightened obediently. I sat back.

Thirty seconds passed.

"Right rein," I said again, slightly less muttered.

Cosimo's ear flicked back toward me in a gesture of attentive acknowledgement that I found, under the circumstances, insufficient.

---

We reached the bend.

It is a gentle bend—a long, easy right-hand curve of the kind that normally requires nothing more than a slight weight shift and a soft feel on the right rein. I have navigated it, on various conveyances, several hundred times. It has never before required a formal announcement.

Today, I felt one was warranted.

"RIGHT!" I called, with the clear, carrying projection of a man who has been issuing commands all morning and still believes, against mounting evidence, that volume is the relevant variable.

Cosimo heard *right*.

What Cosimo understood by *right* was not what I understood by *right*. What Cosimo understood by *right* was a *shoulder-in* — a classical movement, highly regarded in dressage circles, in which the horse's shoulders step inward across the line of travel while the haunches continue on the original track. The horse moves on three hoof-prints instead of two, legs crossing with a precise, diagonal elegance, and the whole effect, performed correctly, is one of considerable athleticism and obedience.

It is the kind of movement that, in the *manège*, earns a nod from a serious trainer.

On a gravel road with a loaded wagon and a gentle right-hand bend that simply wanted navigating, it deposited the nearside wheel into a ditch with a dignified and unhurried certainty.

The wagon tipped. Not catastrophically—it was more of a lean, an expressive lean, a lean that communicated clearly that things were not quite right. Barnaby and Blue stopped immediately, with the matter-of-fact composure of animals who have learned that stopping is usually the correct response to a wagon leaning. Thunderbolt attempted to surge forward into the new situation, was checked by his traces, and began treading in place with the frustrated energy of a horse who wanted very much to do something but couldn't identify what.

Duchess completed her *shoulder-in*, stepped neatly off the road into the verge, and stood there looking composed and faintly expectant, as though awaiting the next instruction.

I climbed down. I inspected the wheel. The ditch was shallow, the wheel undamaged, the load—already somewhat rearranged by the morning's adventures—slightly more rearranged. I climbed back up.

Three minutes.

"Fine," I said, to the team in general. "Fine. That was a miscommunication. We will not use 'right.'"

Barnaby's near ear rotated toward me and back again, like a weathervane checking and discarding a wind direction.

---

We rejoined the road. I shook the reins with the measured optimism of a man recalibrating his approach, not abandoning it. The team advanced. The bend, now behind us, receded into the category of events that had occurred and would not recur.

Ahead, the road ran broadly and straightforwardly toward the market quarter for perhaps half a mile before branching left toward the square. Straightforward. Uncomplicated. All I had to do was go forward.

I would not use "right."

What I had not fully considered was how many occasions might arise, in half a mile of road, that would require the use of "left."

The answer, it transpired, was three.

The first occasion was a hay-wain stopped across two-thirds of the road, its driver deep in conversation with a man at a field gate who showed no signs of concluding the exchange before midwinter. I needed to steer the team left around it. I said *"LEFT!"* at a reasonable volume.

Cosimo and Duchess executed a *travers*.

A *travers*—sometimes called "quarters-in"—is a lateral movement in which the horse travels forward while holding its haunches displaced inward, the body at a slight angle to the direction of travel, the inside legs crossing in front of the outside legs. It is a movement that demonstrates suppleness, engagement, and responsiveness to the leg. In the correct setting, it is a pleasure to observe.

In the present setting, it moved the wagon at a smooth diagonal toward a hawthorn hedgerow at a pace that could only be described as stately.

I hauled on the reins. The pair softened out of the movement with professional grace. Barnaby and Blue, who had been navigating the problem in their own laconic fashion, stepped around the hay-wain without incident. Thunderbolt attempted to bite the hay-wain as we passed. I pretended not to notice.

The second occasion was a wet patch of road, where I wanted the team slightly left of centre to keep the wagon wheels off the soft verge. I said *"Left"* at a considerably lower volume, the tone of a man employing careful modulation.

The result was a half-sized *travers*—Cosimo and Duchess apparently responding to the reduced volume with a proportionally reduced movement, as though the command were a dial they could adjust. This was, I grudgingly noted, almost sensible. The wagon went somewhat left of centre. The verge was avoided. The success was incomplete but real.

I filed this as progress.

The third occasion was not a road condition but a dog—a large, foolish, enthusiastic farm dog who emerged from a gap in a wall and addressed the leading pair with the full force of his opinions at close range. I shouted *"LEFT! LEFT! GET LEFT!"* with the urgency of a man who very much wanted five horses to step promptly away from a loud and sudden threat.

Cosimo and Duchess stepped away promptly to the left, yes. They stepped away in a synchronized *travers* so technically precise that the labourers eating their midday meal on the low wall by the field gate—who had, until that moment, been watching my progress with the comfortable attention of people observing weather—visibly straightened with interest.

The dog, undeterred, pursued at a volume that was genuinely impressive for his size.

Thunderbolt lost his composure entirely and surged left into Cosimo. Cosimo absorbed this with tolerant professionalism and drifted left an extra two inches. The wagon's wheel found the soft verge. We shuddered to an uneven stop.

The dog, satisfied, departed.

"Still faster," I said, under my breath, into the quiet. Nobody heard me. I was grateful for this.

---

A group of farm labourers—four of them, as I counted—were eating their lunch on the low wall with the patient, unrushed appetite of men who have nowhere to be until the afternoon. They had been there for some time. They had, therefore, witnessed a considerable portion of the preceding events.

I was aware of them in the way one is aware of an audience one would prefer not to have.

I gathered the reins. The wagon was back on the road. The team was, with adjustments, facing the right direction. I would get us to the square in one coordinated, purposeful advance if it was the last thing I did. I needed to proceed. I needed, in fact, only one thing from the team.

I needed them to *move*.

"MOVE!" I called, with the full authority of a man who has been in charge of this expedition since dawn and intends to remain so.

Duchess moved.

Duchess moved in *passage*.

The *passage* is a classical dressage movement of the highest difficulty—a slow, cadenced, elevated trot in which each diagonal pair of legs is raised and suspended for a fraction longer than in a normal trot, producing a floating, hovering quality that makes the horse appear to be trotting through something slightly denser than air. It requires years of systematic work to develop. It is considered one of the great achievements of classical equitation. Watching a horse perform true passage is, I am told by people who think about these things, a moving experience.

For Duchess, at this particular moment, it was simply the answer to *MOVE*. The signal matched something deep and ingrained, and she produced it with the automatic completeness of a musician playing a phrase she has rehearsed ten thousand times.

She rose and floated and stepped, one foreleg elevated, then the other, her neck arched, her tail carried high, every element of the movement exactly correct. The wagon, which weighs rather more than Duchess had perhaps accounted for in her calculations, advanced at the speed of a man walking with some reluctance.

Cosimo, beside her, felt the change in her gait through the harness and matched it with the instinctive solidarity of a performer who knows his partner's cue. He too came into *passage*, slightly less elevated than Duchess but no less committed.

Two circus horses. One beautifully matched floating trot. One wagon advancing at perhaps a quarter of the pace of a brisk walk.

The labourer on the left began, slowly and without mockery, to clap.

It was the kind of slow clapping that begins as appreciation and becomes, without changing its tempo, commentary. The man beside him stopped chewing. The two others turned from their bread to watch with the focused attention of men who have recognised something unexpectedly good.

"She's magnificent, that grey," one of them said, with the authority of a man making a fair assessment.

A child appeared from somewhere behind the wall. Then two more children. Apparently the word had got out.

I sat on the bench and watched five horses not making adequate speed toward a market that was, by my pocket-watch, closing in on its final hour with the remorseless efficiency of a thing that does not care about my situation. I watched Duchess float and Cosimo drift and Barnaby and Blue plod behind them with their customary dignity, and Thunderbolt—Thunderbolt, who had been the subject of Bad Decision Number One and had given me nothing but grief since sunrise—was, at this precise moment, walking in a completely straight line at a perfectly ordinary pace, ears forward, reins slack, a horse simply going to market.

I did not know what to do with this information.

"HOLD THAT PACE! STRAIGHT! ADVANCE! ALL OF YOU—*FORWARD!*"

What came out of my mouth was not a command. It was a sentence—a long, exasperated, full-throated declaration that encompassed my feelings about the morning, the horses, the lanes, the hay-wain, the dog, the ditch, the cabbages currently absent from my inventory, the outer road I had declined to use on the grounds of efficiency, and the market bell that I could now, faintly, hear in the distance.

Cosimo, who had been attending to every sound from the bench with the professional alertness of a horse trained to respond to voice cues, found within my declaration something that approximated a familiar signal.

He halted.

He lowered his head, slowly, until his nose was near the ground.

He extended his near foreleg forward in a long, elegant sweep.

He bowed.

It was a complete bow—unhurried, balanced, deeply practised, and accomplished with a grace that made my own posture, hunched on the bench above him, seem by comparison entirely inadequate. He held it for a moment, head low, weight back, the pose of an animal that has done this before a great many people and found it to be very well received.

The labourer who had been clapping stopped.

Then he began again, slower.

One of the children said, in a high, clear voice that carried across the road with perfect acoustic precision: *"He's very good, that dark one."*

I said nothing.

I sat with the reins slack in my hands and Cosimo's bow filling my entire field of vision, and I said nothing at all for what felt like a very long time but was probably four or five seconds.

---

The town was quiet, in the way that a place goes quiet when you stop adding noise to it.

I looked at the five horses arranged before me and, for the first time that morning, I looked at them rather than through them—not as a collection of problems to be corrected, but as a collection of animals doing exactly what they were.

Barnaby and Blue stood in their traces with the deep, impervious patience of animals who have never once in their working lives been the source of any trouble at all and knew it. Thunderbolt stood at the right trace, winded from a morning he had not been designed for, his nervous energy finally exhausted into something like stillness. He had wanted open ground and a single instruction. He had got narrow lanes and five conflicting sets of harness.

Cosimo, rising out of his bow with unhurried dignity, returned to a square halt and waited.

Duchess continued her *passage* for three more elevated strides, realised the forward motion had ceased, and settled. She stood with the poll-high attentiveness of a horse expecting further cues, ready for the next signal, blameless and professional and entirely elsewhere.

I turned it over quietly, the way you turn something over when you are not yet ready to look at it directly.

They were not failing me.

I had assembled five different answers to five different questions and asked them all the same question at the same time, and I was surprised that the conversation had become difficult. Thunderbolt had never been a cart horse. Barnaby and Blue had never been fast horses. Cosimo and Duchess had never been road horses. They were each of them exactly what they were, precisely where they'd been put, doing exactly what their training and their nature and their long experience told them to do.

The question, it was beginning to occur to me—quietly, without fanfare, the way conclusions arrive when you are too tired to argue with them—was whether any of that was their fault.

"You are not wrong, Cosimo," I said, to no one in particular, since the labourers were out of earshot and Cosimo, in any case, had not claimed to be wrong. "You are just somewhere else entirely."

It was not much. It was the most honest thing I had said since leaving the farm.

I picked up the reins—not with the sharp, urgent gather of a man issuing fresh orders, but with a quieter economy, the way you pick up something you have been holding carelessly. I took up Cosimo's left rein by a degree and held it there, not pulling, just maintaining a conversation. He softened into it and straightened by one bearing-point. I held. He held. We agreed, without shouting, where straight was.

I asked Duchess forward with a light feel. She stepped out at a walk—a real walk, not a passage, a working walk that covered ground. I exhaled.

The team moved.

---

It was not fast. I will not pretend it was fast. But it was forward, and it was, after the better part of an hour, forward without incident.

The market square appeared in increments above the rooftops—first the top of the clock tower, then the flag, then the edge of the great hall. I could see the awnings of the outer stalls. I could hear, distantly, the noise of commerce: voices, wheels on cobblestone, the particular hum of a market in its final, urgent hour.

I checked my pocket-watch.

The margin was, by any optimistic reading of the situation, slim. By a pessimistic reading, it was worse than slim. Somewhere in the square, the buyer for my autumn preserves—who had corresponded twice and was, by his own letter, departing on the afternoon coach—was either still at the stalls or was not. I did not know which.

I did not know what I would find at the fork ahead, where the road split left toward the square's main entrance and right toward the quieter side gate. The left road was closer. The left road was, if memory served, also the route the afternoon's incoming parade was scheduled to use, the one the town council had blocked for foot traffic since before dawn.

I had not thought about the parade.

I was thinking about it now.

The rooftops drew nearer. The clock tower showed the time. Somewhere ahead, clearly and without ambiguity, the market bell began to ring.

Cosimo, to his credit, went straight.
