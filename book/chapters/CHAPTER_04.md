### Chapter 4: Outskirts Truce and the Circus Temptation

The road along the town's edge is, I will grudgingly admit, a fine piece of road.

It is wide enough for two wagons to pass with room for a civil exchange of nods. The surface is well-compacted gravel, maintained by the municipality with an enthusiasm that the lanes of the old quarter evidently do not inspire. There are no geese. There are no copper pots. There are no narrow stone pinches with the gall to call themselves thoroughfares. There is simply road—broad, quiet, and almost offensively sensible—running in a long, open arc between the common ground and the mill.

I had, of course, considered this road earlier. I had ruled it out on the grounds of efficiency.

I did not say this aloud.

Barnaby and Blue found their rhythm within thirty paces, settling back into the deep, tectonic *draft* that is their natural condition, like rivers returning to their beds after a flood. Even Thunderbolt—who had spent the better part of the last hour treating every market-day sound as a personal affront—gradually ceased his *treading*. His ears came forward for the first time all morning, tilting toward the distant smell of grass and the blessed absence of tinsmithery.

I sat back on the bench and took stock.

The load was somewhat diminished from its original glory. A quantity of spring greens had been donated, involuntarily, to the cobblestones of Coppergate Lane. One crate had developed a philosophical lean that I had addressed with a length of rope and a great deal of optimism. The horses were winded but sound. I was dusty, slightly hoarse from shouting, and in possession of a considerable quantity of unconverted energy that I had not yet found a productive outlet for.

I turned the situation over in my mind with the careful attention of a craftsman examining a piece of work that has not quite come out as planned.

The problem, I concluded, was not the *concept*. The concept was sound. Three horses pull more than two; therefore three horses are better than two. This is geometry, not guesswork. The difficulty—and I could see it clearly now, in the calm of the open road—was that Barnaby and Blue, for all their considerable merits, were not horses that responded well to *command*. They were self-directed. Methodical. They did what they had always done and they did it at the pace they had always done it, and no amount of instruction from the bench would alter this by so much as a hoof-beat.

What I needed was not just power, and not just speed. What I needed was *obedience*. Horses that *listened*. Horses trained to respond to their handler with precision and immediacy, so that the team moved as a single, coherent instrument rather than as three separate animals with three separate opinions.

I sat a little straighter.

This was, I felt, a significant analytical breakthrough.

It was at this point that I noticed the circus.

---

It had set up on the common ground beyond the mill—a sprawling, colourful arrangement of tents and wagons and ropes and banners that had appeared, seemingly overnight, with the cheerful improbability of a dream. From the road I could see the large main tent, its canvas striped in red and gold, and beyond it a row of smaller pavilions and the neat, orderly lines of the animal enclosures. From somewhere within, a brass band was rehearsing something upbeat and slightly off-tempo that drifted across the common ground with the warm, muffled quality of music heard through canvas walls.

I could smell sawdust and animal feed and, faintly, the particular combination of hay and lamp oil and *something else*—the smell of a working stable that is very well kept. The smell of trained horses.

I have attended travelling circuses in my time. I know their horses. Circus horses are not workhorses, and they are emphatically not racing horses. They are something altogether more refined: *schooled* animals, trained from young age to respond to the most precise of cues with the most complete of obedience. A schooled horse does not decide how fast to walk. It does not choose its own rhythm. It executes instructions—leg, weight, voice—with a responsiveness that a well-tuned instrument would envy.

I pulled the team to a gentle halt at the common-ground gate and climbed down from the bench.

"Barnaby," I said, looping his lead rope to the gate post, "I am about to improve our situation considerably."

Barnaby's ear moved approximately three degrees in my direction. I chose to interpret this as enthusiasm.

---

I found Ambrose near the horse lines, which is exactly where I expected to find him, because Ambrose is a man who is fundamentally always near his horses. He is a large, cheerful figure with a voice that carries across a tent of five hundred without apparent effort—the voice of a man for whom projection is not a skill but a resting state. He had his sleeves rolled and was overseeing the grooming of a grey mare with the focused contentment of someone doing something he genuinely loves.

He saw me coming and his face opened into a broad grin.

"I thought I heard something unusual from the direction of the old quarter," he said, gripping my hand with the enthusiasm of a man who has missed good company. "Something resembling a wagon navigating its way through a confined space using primarily noise. That was you, wasn't it?"

"There were extenuating circumstances," I said. "How is business?"

"Excellent, since you ask. We added a second rope act in the spring and the horses are at their absolute finest. Cosimo alone is worth—but never mind. You have the expression of a man who has come with a proposition, and I have the expression of a man who is already slightly worried about it."

I laid the situation before him with the directness that I believe is the mark of honest commerce between friends. My team was mismatched. My deadline was pressing. His horses were, by reputation and personal observation, the finest trained animals in the region. A temporary loan, for the afternoon's market run. He would have first selection at my stall and a bottle from the cellar—the good vintage, not the one I keep at the front for visitors.

Ambrose listened with his arms folded and a faint smile that I have known for thirty years and have never quite been able to read.

"They're remarkable animals," he said, when I had finished. "Cosimo and Duchess in particular. You won't find a better-schooled pair in three counties."

"Exactly what I—"

"They're trained to cues, not road commands." He said it pleasantly, in the tone of a man stating a simple fact rather than raising an objection. "They'll follow a signal like a shadow follows the sun—but only if the signal is one they know. Shout 'LEFT' at Cosimo and you'll get a very impressive *half-pass*. Whether it takes you where you want to go is another matter entirely." He paused. "And Duchess has a *passage* step so deeply trained that she produces it at the faintest trigger. In the arena, it's breathtaking. On a gravel road with a loaded wagon, I confess I have some questions."

I absorbed this information in the manner of a man who has heard an important caveat and is already translating it into something more manageable.

"So the cues are specific," I said. "I simply won't use those cues."

"That," said Ambrose, with a careful neutrality, "is one approach."

We agreed terms. The good vintage it was, plus first choice of the turnips. Ambrose called one of his handlers, and within twenty minutes, Cosimo and Duchess were being led from the horse lines.

---

They were, I must say, magnificent.

Cosimo was a dark bay—almost black in the shade, with a coat that caught the light like polished mahogany and a bearing that suggested he was very comfortable being admired. He walked with a collected, precise economy of movement, each step placed with the quiet certainty of an animal that has been told, and has internalized, exactly where its feet should be.

Duchess was a dapple grey, lighter-built than Cosimo but no less impressive—her neck arched in a natural *poll-high* carriage that made her look perpetually alert and ready. She moved with a slight extra elevation in her stride, a barely-perceptible floating quality, as though the ground was an obstacle she was tolerating rather than using.

Barnaby watched them approach with the expression of a horse who has seen many things in his years of service and has learned to reserve judgement. Blue simply stepped sideways a few inches, regarded them briefly, and returned to his contemplation of the middle distance.

Thunderbolt was immediately fascinated by Duchess in a way that I judged to be unhelpful and chose to address with a sharp word and a shortening of his trace.

I studied the new pair with a professional eye. Cosimo stood with an almost imperceptible lean—barely noticeable, more a tendency than a posture—to the left. *Years of circling the arena*, some quiet part of my equestrian experience noted, filing the observation under "minor" without further consideration. Duchess lifted her near foreleg slightly higher than her off foreleg at the walk, a subtle asymmetry that on any other horse I would have investigated, but which on this horse, given her training, I recognised as the faint ghost of her schooled elevation.

I noted both. I did not dwell on either.

The integration of five horses into a working *span* is, theoretically, a straightforward matter of geometry and harness allocation. In practice, it requires patience, careful adjustment of the traces to distribute the load evenly, and an honest assessment of which animals can be placed where without causing a temperamental incident.

I completed the harnessing in good order, stood back, and looked at the result.

Five horses. Two massive cold-bloods providing the foundation of power. A racing thoroughbred on the right trace, now somewhat subdued by the afternoon's events. Two circus-trained animals, schooled to precision, ready to provide the obedient leadership that would bind the whole team into the coherent, responsive unit I had always envisioned.

It looked, I felt, extremely capable.

"Right then," I said, climbing back onto the bench and gathering five sets of reins with both hands. "Let us not waste any more of the morning."

"It is the afternoon," said Ambrose, from the gate, with the gentle precision of a man who keeps accurate time.

I chose not to engage with this point. I gave the command to advance.

The team moved out. Five horses, a full wagon, the gravel road ahead. For thirty seconds—perhaps a little more—it was everything I had imagined. Cosimo and Duchess stepped forward with polished authority. Barnaby and Blue settled into their deep pull behind them. Even Thunderbolt managed a creditable forward walk without lurching or shying.

Thirty seconds. Maybe thirty-five.

Then Cosimo began to drift left.

It was not dramatic. It was not a shy or a lurch or any of the sudden, emphatic misbehaviours I had already catalogued from this morning's adventures. It was quiet, gradual, and utterly relentless—the patient, practiced leftward inclination of a horse whose entire working life has been organised around a circle, and who, in the absence of any other strong instruction, will always, given time, find his way back to it.

The wagon began to angle, almost imperceptibly, toward the left verge.

I adjusted the rein. Cosimo softened, straightened for a moment, then began again—left, always left, with the calm inevitability of a compass needle seeking north.

"Hm," I said, to no one in particular.

Behind me, I was fairly certain I could hear Ambrose, still at the gate, not saying anything. The silence of a man who has given fair warning and is now waiting with professional interest to see what happens next.

I squared my shoulders, shortened Cosimo's left rein, and turned the team toward the market square. We had time. We had five horses. We had a plan.

The plan had a slight leftward lean to it, but I felt confident this could be managed.
