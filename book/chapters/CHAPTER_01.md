### Chapter 1: The Slow Morning at Muddy Gate Farm

The sun had yet to clear the horizon, but already I was beset by the tyranny of the ticking pocket-watch. At Muddy Gate Farm, we don’t measure time by the gentle rotation of the stars, but by the cold, hard reality of the Tuesday market. My wagon was loaded to the very gunwales—a substantially heavy load of turnips, crisp cabbages, and early spring greens that sat so low the *reach*—that sturdy wooden beam connecting my axles—groaned under the strain. Even the *leaf springs*, those curved steel plates designed to absorb the jolts of the road, were flattened nearly straight, a testament to the bounty of my humble plots.

I stood by the hitch, watching Barnaby and Blue. They are fine specimens of "cold-bloods"—large, heavy-set draft horses with thick *cannons* (the bone between the knee and the fetlock) and sturdy *pasterns*. They are built for the long, slow haul, possessed of a draft capacity that could move a mountain if given enough sandwiches. But this morning, their rhythmic, unhurried gait felt like a personal affront.

"At this pace," I muttered, adjusting a hames strap with a sharp tug, "the turnips will sprout, go to seed, and start their own competing farm before we even crest the first hill!"

I was vexed by the leisurely tempo of the heavy-set. To my mind, their steady rhythm wasn't "reliable"; it was "plodding." I needed more than just raw power today; I needed velocity.

"Moving a bit slow today, aren't you, neighbor?" 

I looked up to see Silas leaning over his stone wall. Silas has significant experience with the land, but he possesses a temperament as sluggish as my team.

"Speed is a fickle friend," Silas remarked, his eyes tracking the way my wagon settled into the soft earth. "A heavy load cares little for a fast heart; it only bows to a strong back. You'd do well to remember that before you try to outrun the morning."

I dismissed him with a wave of my glove. Silas is a man of the old school, who believes that if a journey doesn't take all day, you haven't done it right. I, however, am a man of optimization. I believe that apparent qualities—the look of speed, the fire of a quick heart—must surely translate into expected results.

My eyes drifted across the fence to the neighboring paddock. There, silhouetted against the rising mist, stood a creature of entirely different "hot-blooded" stock. A racing horse—sleek, narrow-chested, and vibrating with a nervous energy that suggested he found the very act of standing still to be a Herculean task.

A brilliant, mathematical equation formed in my mind: Raw Power (Barnaby and Blue) + Raw Speed (The High-Stepper) = Market Dominance. 

It was a perfect optimization. Why use a choir when you could add a trumpet?

I reached into the shadows of the barn for the spare harness—the one with the lighter leather and the sharper clink. As I approached the team, I could see Barnaby’s ear flick back in a confused arc. They only ever needed two sets of leather. But today, I was changing the music. 

The "Fidgety One" was done with the slow dance. It was time for a race.
