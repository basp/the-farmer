### Chapter 3: Bad Decision #2: The Twisty Lanes to Town

The lanes, I had decided, were a stroke of genius.

Where the outer road swings wide around the old quarter—a sensible, broad arc of well-compacted gravel, blissfully free of hazards—the twisty lanes cut a bold, direct path right through the heart of market-day activity. Shorter, in the most literal, as-the-crow-flies sense of the word. And the crow, I should point out, does not have three tons of winter vegetables and an overexcitable racing horse to contend with.

But that was a detail.

"A straight line is fastest," I informed Barnaby, as I steered us away from the wide outer road and pointed the team toward the mouth of Coppergate Lane. "If you ignore the bumps."

Barnaby said nothing. He had developed a talent for a very specific silence—a wide, deep, *prehistoric* silence—that he deployed whenever I announced a new plan.

We entered the lanes.

It began innocuously enough. The cobblestones were indeed firm beneath the hooves—I permitted myself a small, private nod of satisfaction—and for three full seconds, the enterprise felt like a triumph. Then the world woke up.

A flock of geese belonging to old Merchant Hobbs came around the first bend with the force and confidence of a cavalry charge. They had, apparently, the same destination as myself and absolutely no intention of yielding the right of way. Barnaby and Blue, seasoned veterans of the road, slowed with long-suffering patience. Thunderbolt, for whom standing still was already a philosophical crisis, attempted to vault sideways into a cheese merchant's barrow.

"HOLD!" I bellowed at the reins. The geese, unimpressed, honked back.

Geese, I should note, are the most *mulish* creatures in the farmyard—more stubborn even than the ill-tempered mule, because the mule at least has the decency to stand still when it is being obstructive. Geese *advance*. Slowly. With tremendous confidence.

We lost two minutes to the geese.

The lane opened briefly, and I shook the reins with renewed optimism. "Still faster!" I announced to no one in particular.

The tinsmith's stall appeared on the left. He was, at that precise moment, testing the acoustics of what appeared to be an especially resonant copper pot—tapping it with a small hammer in a series of bright, ringing blows that echoed beautifully off the narrow stone walls. For Thunderbolt, who had been managing his anxiety by focusing intensely on the haunches of the horse in front of him, this was an ambush.

He shied—a sudden, explosive sideways leap that is the hot-blood's answer to any stimulus above the level of mild conversation. The *traces* sang under the jerk, the wagon slewed hard left, and a crate of spring greens gave a long, dignified wobble before depositing roughly half its contents across the cobblestones.

"You'll pay for those!" I shouted, though at whom exactly was unclear—the tinsmith, Thunderbolt, or the laws of physics generally.

"Try the outer road, friend!" called a woman from above, hanging washing from an upstairs window with the serene confidence of someone watching events from a safe elevation. "It's wider!"

"I'm *aware* of the outer road!" I replied, gathering the reins again. "I have *chosen* not to use it!"

The lane narrowed. This was, in hindsight, information I had failed to properly account for. I had traversed these lanes on foot many times, and on those occasions they had seemed quite adequate. But those occasions had not involved a wagon, a mismatched span of three horses—two of them the approximate dimensions of river barges—and an increasing number of market-day pedestrians who had developed an urgent need to press themselves flat against the walls as we passed.

Children found the whole affair delightful. They ran alongside, calling out encouragement and commentary in equal measure, pointing at the wobbling load, at Thunderbolt's wild eyes, at the trail of scattered greens marking our route like a very unfortunate breadcrumb path. One small boy, no older than seven, jogged beside the wagon for a full twenty paces shouting "He's going sideways! He's going SIDEWAYS!" with the giddy authority of a sports commentator.

He was not wrong. The combination of Thunderbolt's surging and checking, the cobbled camber of the lane, and the increasingly frantic geometry of my steering had begun to give the whole outfit a faintly *crabwise* quality—the wagon travelling in a general forward direction but with an eerie lateral drift, like a vessel fighting a crosswind.

"MOVE! HOLD! LEFT! NO—*STEADY*!"

My commands had evolved from instructions into a kind of desperate percussion. Barnaby and Blue responded with their customary measured dignity, making small, correct adjustments as though quietly compensating for a navigational instrument they had long stopped trusting. Thunderbolt responded to everything by doing more of whatever he was already doing, only faster.

We came around the sharpest bend in Coppergate—the one locals call the Elbow, for reasons that were now achingly apparent—at a speed that was simultaneously too fast to be safe and too slow to be impressive. The right-hand wheel clipped the corner of an ironmonger's display, producing a magnificent cascade of horseshoes that rang across the cobbles like dropped coins. The ironmonger shouted something that was mostly obscured by the clanging, but his meaning was clear enough.

"STILL FASTER!" I announced, with the hollow conviction of a man who has stopped believing his own argument but cannot yet identify a graceful exit.

Then we reached the pinch—a stretch where the buildings lean toward each other overhead, narrowing the lane to a width that I can only describe as *aspirational* for a wagon of my dimensions. We stopped. Completely. A vegetable barrow on the left, a cartload of timber that had stalled coming the other direction on the right, and between them a gap that a geographer might have called "snug."

Three minutes passed.

The farmer from the timber cart and I regarded each other across the impasse. He had the expression of a man who had made one bad choice and arrived at his consequence. I recognised the look. I was wearing the same one.

After some careful manoeuvring, some frank words between professionals, and the temporary relocation of the vegetable barrow by its very patient owner, we squeezed through. The lane finally opened out at the far end of Coppergate, where the old buildings fall back and the road runs level and broad along the town's edge—the quiet, unhurried fringe that precedes the market square.

The horses breathed. Barnaby shook his great head, jangling his *hames* with the slow, deliberate patience of a man setting down a very heavy box. Blue exhaled a long, rumbling breath through his nose. Even Thunderbolt stood still for a moment, his sides heaving, his nervous energy—temporarily, miraculously—spent.

An elderly man sat on a low wall nearby, eating his lunch with the unhurried composure of someone entirely untroubled by the events he had just witnessed.

"Took the lanes, did you?" he observed.

"I did," I said.

"Faster, was it?"

I looked back at Coppergate Lane, at the scattered greens, the trail of horseshoes, the small boy still waving in the middle distance. I looked at the scattered remains of what had been a tidy load. I checked my pocket-watch.

"It was *direct*," I said firmly.

The old man nodded slowly, in the way that very wise people nod when they have decided that a man must be left to his own conclusions.

I straightened in the bench and looked toward the market square, still some distance ahead. The team was calmer now, settled by the open air and the brief rest. Even Thunderbolt seemed steadied—though I noticed his ears were still swivelling, cataloguing threats from every direction with the vigilance of a creature who had learned, through painful experience, that the world was full of copper pots.

What we needed, I decided, was not different horses on the wrong road, but different horses altogether. Horses that were *trained*. Disciplined. Horses that followed *commands* rather than their own startled instincts.

I looked ahead to where the road curved past the broad, colourful canopies I had noticed on our way through the outskirts. The travelling circus had set up on the common ground beyond the mill—a bright, cheerful sprawl of tents and wagons and, if I remembered correctly, some of the most rigorously trained animals in the region.

I sat a little straighter.

The situation was still retrievable. I simply needed to *upgrade* the team.
