### Chapter 2: Bad Decision #1: Borrowing Thunderbolt

Bringing Thunderbolt into the team was, on paper, a masterstroke of logistical engineering. He was a "hot-blood" of the finest pedigree—sleek, narrow-chested, and possessing a tail set so high it looked like a decorative plume. Beside the massive, earth-bound frames of Barnaby and Blue, he looked like a silver needle threaded through two blocks of granite. 

As I cinched the lighter leather harness onto him, Thunderbolt was already *treading*—a nervous, rapid-fire stepping that suggested his internal clock was set to "gallop" while the rest of the world was stuck on "saunter." Barnaby watched with a single, skeptical eye flicked backward, his heavy ear twitching in a rhythmic arc that seemed to say, *This won't end well.*

"Nonsense, Barnaby," I muttered, tightening a buckle. "It’s simple math. We’re adding velocity to mass."

I climbed onto the bench, took up the reins, and gave the signal. 

The result was immediate, dramatic, and entirely unhelpful. Thunderbolt surged forward with a burst of raw speed that would have been impressive on a racetrack. But here, hitched to a wagon whose *reach* was already groaning under three tons of winter vegetables, he was less of a motor and more of a twitch. 

The wagon didn't accelerate; it suffered a series of violent, bone-shaking spasms. Thunderbolt would lunge, his *traces* snapping taut with a sound like a pistol shot, only to be yanked back by the immovable weight of the load and the steady, unbothered rhythm of the workhorses. It was like tying a fiddle solo to a church bell and calling it a marching band. Barnaby and Blue continued their deep, powerful *draft*, their massive hooves striking the earth in a slow, tectonic beat, while Thunderbolt danced and checked beside them, a frantic trumpet blaring out of time with the rest of the choir.

"Hold the line, you nervous scrap of string!" I shouted, as the uneven torque began to make the front axle shimmy.

We hit a minor rut—a mere pebble by Muddy Gate standards—but with Thunderbolt’s sudden lurch, it became a catastrophe. The wagon slewed sideways, the *leaf springs* shrieking in protest. Behind me, I heard the sickening *thump-thump-roll* of crates shifting. A dozen prime cabbages vaulted over the tailboard, bouncing down the road like green, leafy cannonballs.

I pulled the team to a jagged halt near the edge of the paddock. Old Tom, a retired groom with skin like cured leather, was leaning against a post, watching the carnage with a slow, deliberate chew of his tobacco.

"Speed is a dear thing when bought in foolishness, neighbor," Tom called out, his voice as dry as hay. "A plow team is a choir, not a trumpet contest. You’ve got a lot of brass there, but you’re losing the melody."

"The melody is fine!" I snapped, wiping sweat from my brow and pointedly ignoring the trail of cabbages behind us. "The problem isn't the horse; it’s the road. This main track is too soft for a quick step."

I looked toward the town, where the wide, sensible outer road curved away. Then I looked at the "shortcuts"—the narrow, twisty lanes that cut through the old quarter. If the road was the problem, I would simply change the road.

"We're taking the lanes," I declared to the skeptical ears of my team. "More stone, less mud. That'll fix the rhythm."

Barnaby’s ear flicked back again. I chose to interpret it as agreement.
